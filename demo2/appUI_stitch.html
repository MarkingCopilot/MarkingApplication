Proof by Induction

By Bob Williams

Theorem: For any integer $n \ge 1$, the sum of the first $n$ positive integers is given by the formula:

$S_n = \sum_{i=1}^{n} i = \frac{n(n+1)}{2}$

Proof: We will use mathematical induction.

Base Case: For $n=1$, the left side is $\sum_{i=1}^{1} i = 1$. The right side is $\frac{1(1+1)}{2} = \frac{2}{2} = 1$. The formula holds for $n=1$.

Inductive Hypothesis: Assume that for some integer $k \ge 1$, the formula holds. That is, assume:

$\sum_{i=1}^{k} i = \frac{k(k+1)}{2}$

Inductive Step: We must show that the formula also holds for $n=k+1$. We want to prove:

$\sum_{i=1}^{k+1} i = \frac{(k+1)((k+1)+1)}{2} = \frac{(k+1)(k+2)}{2}$

We start with the left side of the equation for $n=k+1$:

$\sum_{i=1}^{k+1} i = (\sum_{i=1}^{k} i) + (k+1)$

By the inductive hypothesis, we can substitute the formula for the sum up to $k$:

$= \frac{k(k+1)}{2} + (k+1)$

Now, we find a common denominator to combine the terms:

$= \frac{k(k+1)}{2} + \frac{2(k+1)}{2}$
$= \frac{k(k+1) + 2(k+1)}{2}$

Factor out the common term $(k+1)$ from the numerator:

$= \frac{(k+1)(k+2)}{2}$

This is the right side of the equation for $n=k+1$. Thus, the formula holds for $n=k+1$.

Conclusion: By the principle of mathematical induction, the formula is true for all integers $n \ge 1$.
